How the first page gets shaped

Rarity is not just age. It is significance, provenance, and restraint.

This page shows the same logic we use in the marketplace, in a simple visual form. The goal is not to surface every old Porsche first. The goal is to put the most interesting cars at the top: rare factory builds, special models, air-cooled icons, and unusual PTS cars that feel genuinely special.

What rises to the top

Cars earn points from rarity signals, then we apply guardrails so the list stays curated instead of repetitive.

Top priority Rare factory specification, halo models, classic air-cooled significance.
Secondary lift PTS, low mileage, matching numbers, special packages, and provenance clues.
Guardrails Modern cars are capped, and overrepresented shapes like Speedsters are restrained.

Score logic

We start with the strongest rarity signals first. Then we add supporting details. If two cars are close, the one with deeper significance should stay ahead.

How the ranking behaves

The system is opinionated on purpose. It prefers a few exceptional cars over many similar ones.

1
Start with model significance Rare factory cars and important air-cooled models begin ahead of ordinary listings.
2
Add the good details PTS, matching numbers, original paint, low owner count, and rare options increase the score.
3
Keep modern cars under control Late-model cars can still rank well, but they should not crowd out older icons just because they are newer and shinier.
4
Prevent one shape from dominating Speedsters are capped so they do not appear on every first page and drown out the rest of the selection.

What gets extra credit

Some details are especially powerful when they appear on the right generation of car.

Air-cooled classics

Classic significance gets a bonus. If they also have PTS, homologation, or Turbo heritage, the score compounds.

PTS on old models

Old Porsches with special colors feel more curated and exclusive than a generic modern build.

Special factory builds

RS, RS America, Turbo S, 911R, 959, Singer-grade oddities, and similar cars deserve early placement.

Condition signals

Low miles, original paint, matching numbers, and careful ownership help separate serious cars from ordinary ones.

Examples

A 930 Turbo or 964 Carrera RS can beat a newer ordinary 911 because the older car has more depth. A Speedster can still be high, but it should not occupy every opening slot.

Usually ranks higher Usually ranks lower
964 Carrera RS, 930 Turbo, 959, 911R Ordinary modern 911s with no special specification
Air-cooled PTS cars with honest provenance Late-model cars with only generic low-mileage appeal
Factory rare cars with original paint and matching numbers Common trims with nice photos but no rarity story

The point is not to make the page old-car-only. The point is to make it special-car-first, with enough restraint that one body style does not take over the front page.
